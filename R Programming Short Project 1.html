Rerun this plot, but use the proportion instead. List one advantage of using counts and one advantage of using proportions to compare developments over time.

ggplot(data = my_babyname, aes(x = year, y = prop, colour = year)) + labs(x = "Year", y = "Proportion of baby name 'Maria'" , title = "Proportion of baby name per year (1964-2021)") + theme(plot.title = element_text(hjust = 0.5)) + geom_point() + geom_line() + scale_x_continuous(breaks = seq(from = 1960, to = 2021, by = 10)) + theme_minimal()

- Counts give a very concise idea about the change in the variable over time as we can see the exact number of times a variable has appeared throughout a certain time period.

- Proportions are a good way to see the change over time with respect to the overall change in population. They can also help us see the change in one variable over time given the other features remain consistent. Proportions are also useful in enlarging or reducing an image to scale depending on the nature of the change over time.

Analysing Survey Data: Voting Behaviour during the Brexit Referendum.

This exercise uses data from the British Election Study collected shortly before the Brexit Referendum. It will help us analyse how age and education levels relate to voting “leave” or “remain”. The data are described and used in Sara Hobolt’s paper “The Brexit vote: a divided nation, a divided continent” published in the Journal of European Public Policy in 2016. The dataset is part of the 7th Wave of the British Election Study, constituting the pre-campaign “Panel Survey Study of the 2016 EU Referendum”, conducted before the referendum. The sample size is much larger than usual election studies, allowing for a fine-grained analysis of vote choice based on age and education.
